## Chapter 2: The Tin Island
**Status:** Not built

Phuket's economy in three acts: tin, rubber, tourism.

**Possible structure:** scrollytelling — as the user scrolls, the page transitions through eras. Each era has a hero visual and a short essay.

**Key content:**
- Tin's geological backstory (alluvial deposits in the Andaman granite belt).
- 18th-century tin mining was largely Malay and indigenous.
- 19th-century industrial transformation: Hokkien laborers, Western capital, dredging technology.
- The dredge ships in Phuket Bay (the Tongkah Harbour Tin Dredging Co., 1907 onward).
- Decline post-WWII and especially after the 1985 tin price collapse.
- Rubber as the bridge crop.
- Tourism's takeoff after the 1973 opening of Patong, Club Med 1986, the airport upgrade.

**Visualization ideas:**
- An animated chart showing tin price 1850–2000, with annotations.
- A map of mine locations (many are now lakes — the famous Heroines' Monument area, parts of Phuket Town).
- Old photographs paired with current photos of the same locations.

## Chapter 3: Faiths of the Island
**Status:** Not built

Phuket's religious geography is unusually rich for a Thai island — Theravada Buddhism, Chinese Mahayana/Taoism, Sunni Islam, smaller Hindu and Sikh communities, and the syncretic practices of the Chao Le.

**Possible structure:** filterable map of religious sites with toggle by tradition.

**Key sites to include:**
- **Buddhist:** Wat Chalong (most-visited temple), Wat Phra Thong (the half-buried Buddha), Big Buddha, Wat Phra Nang Sang.
- **Chinese:** Jui Tui Shrine, Bang Neow Shrine, Kathu Shrine (the three main Vegetarian Festival shrines), Sang Tham Shrine.
- **Muslim:** Mukaram Bang Tao Mosque, Surin Mosque, dozens of village mosques in the north.
- **Hindu:** Shri Supramaniam Temple (Phuket Town).
- **Christian:** Phuket Christian Center, the small Catholic chapel.
- **Chao Le animism:** the spirit boats, the boat-floating ceremony at Rawai.

**Verification needed before publishing.** Religious site lists drift; verify with web search.

## Chapter 4: The Vegetarian Festival
**Status:** Not built

A nine-day Taoist festival (the Nine Emperor Gods Festival, 九皇大帝) held in the ninth lunar month. Phuket's version is famous and sometimes notorious — the body-piercing processions, the firewalking, the strict vegan diet.

**Possible structure:** long-form illustrated essay with an embedded calendar showing each day's events.

**Sections:**
- Origins: the 1825 epidemic and the touring Chinese opera troupe story.
- The Nine Emperor Gods (cosmology).
- The strict purification rules (vegan diet, white clothing, abstinence, no meat in the household).
- The mah song (entranced spirit-medium) tradition.
- Each shrine's procession route through Phuket Town.
- Ethical and cultural framing — the body-piercing imagery is visually intense; treat it with seriousness, not exoticism.

**Sensitivity notes:** This is a living religious practice, not a tourist spectacle. Use respectful language. Avoid graphic body-piercing photography unless the user specifically wants it.

## Chapter 6: The 2004 Tsunami
**Status:** Not built

The Indian Ocean tsunami of 26 December 2004 killed approximately 5,400 people in Thailand, with Phuket and Khao Lak among the hardest-hit areas. This chapter must be done carefully.

**Tone:** the calm voice of a memorial museum. Not journalistic, not voyeuristic. Honor the dead, inform the living.

**Possible content:**
- Map of impact zones on Phuket — Patong, Kamala, Kata, Karon all hit; east coast spared.
- Timeline of the morning (earthquake at 7:58 AM Thai time, waves arriving Phuket ~10:30 AM).
- The warning systems built since (sirens, evacuation routes, signage).
- Memorial sites: the Tsunami Memorial in Khao Lak, the rebuilt Patong, plaques.
- A note on what to do if a tsunami warning sounds today.

**Avoid:** photographs of bodies, sensational imagery, body-count framing as the headline.

## Chapter 7: Beaches & Geography
**Status:** Not built

Phuket is a granite island ~50 km long, with a wet rainforest interior, a developed west coast (long sandy beaches), and a quieter east coast (mangroves, mudflats, small coves). Phang Nga Bay's limestone karsts to the northeast are arguably the most spectacular landscape in the country.

**Possible structure:** interactive map with beach filter — by characteristic (sandy/rocky), crowd level, monsoon safety, access difficulty.

**Geographic content:**
- The granite spine and how it shaped the island.
- Monsoon seasons: SW monsoon (May–Oct) makes west-coast swimming dangerous; NE monsoon (Nov–Apr) is the dry "high season."
- The annual rip-current deaths and the red-flag warning system.
- Mangroves on the east coast (and why they matter — fish nurseries, storm protection, the failed shrimp-farm developments).
- Phang Nga Bay karsts and how they formed (limestone uplift + sea-level changes).
- The quiet ecological story: coral bleaching, sea-grass loss, dugong populations.

## Chapter 8: Languages You'll Hear
**Status:** Not built

Walking through Phuket Town in a single afternoon, you might hear: Central Thai (administrative), Southern Thai dialect (local), Hokkien (older Baba families), Burmese (workers), Russian (tourists in the south), English (tourism lingua franca), and occasional Tamil, Hindi, or Malay.

**Possible content:**
- Short audio samples (if possible — record or source CC0 audio).
- Sample phrases in each language.
- A map showing where each is most likely to be heard.
- The story of Phuket Hokkien specifically — it's a distinctive dialect, drifting away from mainland Hokkien, with Thai and Malay loanwords.
- The Southern Thai dialect and how it differs from Bangkok Thai (faster, more emphatic, different particles).
